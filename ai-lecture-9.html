Human vs Machine Language Understanding

Human Understanding

Humans process language through years of experience, cultural context, and intuitive understanding of meaning, emotion, and subtext.

  • Contextual interpretation
  • Emotional understanding
  • Cultural nuances
  • Common sense reasoning

Machine Understanding

Machines process language through statistical patterns, mathematical models, and explicit programming or training on large datasets.

  • Pattern recognition
  • Statistical analysis
  • Rule-based processing
  • Data-driven learning

Text Preprocessing: The Foundation

Before any analysis, raw text must be cleaned and prepared. This critical step affects all downstream tasks.

  • Tokenization: Breaking text into individual words or tokens
  • Cleaning: Removing unwanted characters, HTML tags, special symbols
  • Normalization: Converting to lowercase, handling contractions
  • Stop Words: Removing common words like "the", "and", "is"
  • Stemming/Lemmatization: Reducing words to their root forms
Example: "The quick brown foxes are running!" ‚Üí ["quick", "brown", "fox", "run"]

Text Representation Methods

Traditional Methods

Bag of Words (BoW): Represents text as frequency counts of words, ignoring order and grammar.

  • Simple and interpretable
  • Loses word order information
  • High dimensionality problem

TF-IDF: Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency weights words by their importance across documents.

Advanced Methods

N-grams: Captures local word order by considering sequences of n consecutive words.

  • Bigrams: "New York", "machine learning"
  • Trigrams: "natural language processing"
  • Preserves some context

Vector Embeddings: Dense numerical representations that capture semantic relationships.

Sentiment Analysis: Understanding Emotions

Sentiment analysis determines the emotional tone of text, crucial for understanding public opinion and customer feedback.

  • Binary Classification: Positive vs Negative sentiment
  • Multi-class: Positive, Negative, Neutral classifications
  • Fine-grained: Scale from very negative to very positive
  • Aspect-based: Sentiment toward specific features or topics
Challenge: Sarcasm, context-dependent sentiment, and cultural differences in expression

Level 2: Core NLP

Language Models and Linguistic Analysis

Language Models: Predicting Text

Language models learn the probability distribution of word sequences, enabling them to predict and generate text.

  • Statistical Models: Based on word frequency and co-occurrence patterns
  • N-gram Models: Predict next word based on previous n-1 words
  • Markov Models: Assume future words depend only on recent history
  • Evaluation: Perplexity measures how well model predicts text
P("learning" | "machine") = Count("machine learning") / Count("machine")
Lower perplexity indicates better language modeling performance

Neural Networks and Deep Learning

Word Embeddings: Dense Representations

Word embeddings represent words as dense vectors in continuous space, capturing semantic relationships.

  • Dense Vectors: Typically 100-300 dimensions vs millions for one-hot
  • Semantic Similarity: Similar words have similar vectors
  • Analogies: Vector arithmetic captures relationships
  • Transfer Learning: Pre-trained embeddings boost performance

Attention Mechanisms: Focusing on Relevance

Attention allows models to focus on different parts of the input when generating each output token.
